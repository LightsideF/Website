The Situation

When a handshake isn't enough: the trap of an informal business transfer

I ran the restaurant as a sole trader for more than twenty years. When I retired, my son took over — we did it informally, family to family. He ran the business day-to-day. I walked away. That was the plan.

But here's what neither of us understood: a sole trader and their business are legally the same thing. I had handed over the keys and the day-to-day operation. I had not handed over legal liability. My name was still on the paperwork as the sole trader. The business was still registered in my name.

Years later, HMRC opened a tax investigation into the business. They found irregularities and underreporting. They issued an assessment for almost £450,000 in unpaid income tax, National Insurance contributions, and penalties. Because the sole trader registration was still in my name, the debt was legally mine — regardless of who had actually been running the business, or who had incurred the losses.

My accountant and a specialist in tax investigations did excellent work reducing the amount claimed. They challenged the assessment, and they succeeded in bringing it down materially. But the remaining demand was still more than £300,000. As a pensioner with a closed business, I had no way to pay it. I had never been in financial trouble before. I had paid off my home. I had done everything right. And yet, because of a piece of paperwork I had never thought to update when I stepped back from the business, HMRC could now pursue me personally.

That is when I had to face the reality: if I could not pay the debt, I would be insolvent. And insolvency meant considering options I had never imagined I would need.

What We Did and Why It Worked

The work behind the outcome

When a sole trader dies, retires, or steps away from their business, liability does not automatically transfer. That liability stays with whoever is registered as the sole trader — regardless of who is actually running the business.

In Mr. A's case, his son had been operating the restaurant for years. But HMRC had no legal relationship with his son. The liability chain ran to Mr. A, because Mr. A was the sole trader of record. The solution had to address this structure, not ignore it.

Our work fell into two parts: first, understanding the insolvency options available to him, and second, protecting his home within those options.

We counselled Mr. A and his family on the process of insolvency and what it would mean. We explained how a Trustee in Bankruptcy operates, and what powers they have over assets — and crucially, what the limits of those powers are. A Trustee has an interest in the bankrupt's property, but that interest is not automatic seizure. It has to be realised through a negotiation. That created room to work.

After detailed analysis of the property's equity, the Trustee's likely recovery position, and what the family could realistically raise, we opened negotiations. We positioned an initial offer of £100,000 to buy out the Trustee's interest in the home. The Trustee's solicitors held firm. They even claimed that because Mrs. A was not on the legal title of the property, she had no claim on the equity, which was simply untrue! We kept the negotiations going. After almost twelve months of negotiation, they moved. The Trustee ended up agreeing to accept £94,000 (lower than our initial offer) for a full release of interest in the property.

Mr. A's home was saved. The debt was resolved through the bankruptcy process. He was discharged after twelve months. He had walked away from bankruptcy thinking he would lose everything. He left with his home and his life restored.

Questions about sole trader liability and insolvency

The sole trader remains legally liable for all business debts until a formal transfer has been completed and registered with HMRC. Handing over the day-to-day operation of the business does not transfer the liability. The sole trader can still be pursued for any debts incurred by the business, even if they have stepped away and another person is running it.

A formal sole trader transfer requires:

• Notification to HMRC
• Closure of the old sole trader registration
• The new operator registering as a sole trader (if they are operating as self-employed)
• Or, if the business is being incorporated into a limited company, proper company registration and a handover of assets and liabilities

Without these formal steps, the original owner remains the legal operator of the business in the eyes of HMRC and other creditors. This is what happened to Mr. A. Years after stepping away, he was still liable for debts incurred during his son's operation because the paperwork had never been formally changed.

Yes — if you are the registered owner of a sole trader business, you are personally liable for all debts incurred by that business, regardless of who actually incurred them. That is the defining feature of sole trader liability: the sole trader and the business are legally the same entity.

In Mr. A's case, HMRC pursued him for business debts because he was the registered sole trader. It did not matter that his son had been running the business for years. It did not matter that Mr. A had retired. The law sees the sole trader as responsible for all the business's liabilities.

This is very different from a limited company, where the company itself is the legal entity responsible for its debts, and the director's personal liability is limited. As a sole trader, your personal liability is unlimited. You are personally liable not just for income tax, but for business rates, suppliers' invoices, employees' National Insurance contributions, penalties — everything.

If you have handed over a sole trader business informally, or are thinking of doing so, it is critical to complete a proper legal and tax transfer. If you are trading as a sole trader and you discover significant liabilities, it is equally important to get specialist advice early. The debt can be substantial, but the options — including formal insolvency — can protect you and your assets in ways that passive inaction cannot.

Registered pension funds are generally protected in bankruptcy and do not vest in the Trustee. This protection covers most personal and occupational pensions. However, pension income that you are already drawing — regular pension payments — may be taken into account when the Trustee assesses your income for an Income Payments Agreement. The rules in this area are specific and depend on the type and status of the pension. It is important to take specialist advice on your individual position, and you can discuss this with us.

It depends on whether your home has equity, and who owns the equity. Despite someone being the legal owner of the property, and even being on the mortgage, they may not own the equity. This can arise for many reasons.

In Mr. A's case, he was the sole legal owner of the property, and there was no mortgage. However, we established that his beneficial interest in the property was only 15%, therefore the Trustee in Bankruptcy could not claim the full £600,000, but could only lay claim to a maximum of £150,000. Settlement was eventually agreed at £94,000.

In other situations, if the mortgage is greater than the property's value, the Official Receiver will usually leave the property with you unless you actively choose otherwise. If your home has significant equity, the Trustee will want to realise that value by selling it. A family member or friend can buy it on your behalf, but if they don't, then the Trustee may require you to sell it to realise that equity.

The earlier you seek advice, the better.
